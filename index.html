Model Checking Techniques for Drone Swarms

 

Purpose

Robotic systems of any nature show remarkable promise: their ability to go where humans cannot because of potential dangers, or mortal threats; and their capacity to achieve things not physically possible with human labor (such as in robotic-assisted surgery, or complicated landing sequences).
Moreover, the specialisation of swarm-robotics provides even greater potential. Swarm robotics uses more than one robot to accomplish tasks such as search and rescue, surveillance, and resource harvesting. Since many robots can be used, individual robots do not need exceptional capability, but instead the cumulative capability of the group can be used. This scalability permits easy upgrading as the task size increases, simply by purchasing more robots to integrate into the swarm. Additionally, with multiple agents, it is possible to build in resilience, such that a fault of individual drones does not prevent the task from being completed. By virtue of the scale involved, these will mostly involve autonomous systems - capable of organising themselves and completing tasks without any human input.
A significant requirement for almost all autonomous tasks is the ability for many robots to agree on things. In the basic case, this might be used to agree on the task to complete. More generally, this could be used to agree on the roles of the individual robots in the swarm, or even which task individual robots are assigned to if there are multiple tasks to be done.

Background

Many protocols and swarm designs attempt to satisfy these properties, however, previously it was not possible to verify this behaviour. The need to be able to verify the properties of swarm communication and behaviour lies in the autonomy of the robots. Any potential failure of a robotic swarm should be detected as soon as possible. The robots controlled by these algorithms may be a significant risk if the behaviour becomes unpredictable; the potential for damage to property and life requires very reliable operation. Given the advantages of autonomy, it is likely that some robots are not under constant supervision, given the potential reduction of expenses of robotic autonomy. Therefore, it is likely that there would be a delay before any issues with these robots are detected. To avoid this situation entirely, multiple techniques have been developed in order to verify that the protocols developed cannot fail or break down in such a way. Previous work however has fixated upon swarms of a fixed size, and limited tolerance of faults. This work aims to increase the application to real scenarios, where faults are actually expected and regular occurrences.

Innovation

The developments studied in this research stem from generalising these proofs, such that any size of swarm can be used. This is achieved by parameterising the swarm size. This simply states that the proof will work for any specific swarm size. This permits the proof to reason about all swarm sizes without explicitly proving correctness for each individual swarm size. With this work, it should also be possible to reason about fault tolerance. This permits evaluation of the correctness to say that “with at least 93% for example of robots working correctly, the behaviour will still be correct”.
